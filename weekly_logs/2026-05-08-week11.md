With the collapsed dataset in hand, this week moved into the core model notebook: defining the preprocessing strategy and beginning dimensionality reduction exploration before fitting any predictive model.

The first step was designing a preprocessing plan for the 6 input features. `Color$` (C/M/Y/K) was encoded with One-Hot Encoding rather than label encoding, since there is no ordinal relationship between ink colours. 

`HeadIdx#` (print head index, 1–30) was harder to determine, since it has a close relationship with the physical layout of the printer. It could be considered a categorical value, but that would ignore the existing spatial structure of the printer, and therefore not take into account that chips and nozzles are spatially related and have influence over their neighbours. Following this logic, it was decided to keep them as is, as spatial adjacency gives the ordering genuine physical meaning. To solve the neighbouring nozzle problem, several strategies were considered, such as calculating a coefficient of neighbouring nozzle influence, and brought into consultation with Canon.

The remaining continuous features (`V`, `F_r`, `dt2`, `Coverage#`) were left as passthrough for tree-based methods or flagged for MinMax scaling for distance-based models. Since there is still ongoing research regarding which model fits the data the best, this decision branched into several different explorations of methodologies.

A `ColumnTransformer` was built to apply these transformations, producing a 9-column encoded feature matrix (4 One Hot Encoded colours + 5 numeric features) over 104,340 rows.

PCA was then applied on the encoded input features to assess whether any dimensionality reduction was feasible. The results were immediate and striking: a single component explains **99.89% of variance** in the feature space. Inspecting the PC1 loadings confirmed that `dt2` contributes a loading of ~0.9999, completely dominating the axis. `Coverage#` is a distant second; all other features are negligible. The same experiment was repeated on the 1,120-dimensional waveform output space (both with and without StandardScaler), yielding similar findings — the first component drives nearly all variance, with waveform positions around index 694–749 dominating PC1 and a different cluster around 1106–1010 driving PC2.

An attempt was then made to _normalise out dt2_ by fitting a linear regression predicting `V`, `F_r`, and `Coverage#` from `dt2` and using the residuals as a dt2-corrected feature matrix. The approach ran into a `ValueError` when the residual dataframe was passed back to the preprocessor (which expected `dt2` as a column that no longer existed), leaving the PCA-after-alignment path unfinished. 

The notebook closes with the conclusion that **PCA is not the right solution** for this feature space, and that the next step is to explore a different modelling approach in a follow-up notebook.

## Reflection

The PCA results this week shifted the focus from dimensionality reduction as a modelling technique to `dt2` as a structural concern. A single parameter accounting for 99.89% of feature-space variance is a signal that the feature space is not yet well-formed for modelling. `dt2` behaves more like a calibration setting than a predictive feature, and its wide numerical range relative to the other inputs means variance-based methods will always collapse onto it.

The attempt to normalise out its effect via linear regression residuals was a reasonable direction, though incomplete due to a preprocessing schema mismatch. This points to a broader consideration: the preprocessing pipeline needs to be designed with `dt2`'s special role in mind from the start, rather than treated as one feature among equals. The conclusion that PCA is not appropriate here is itself useful, as it narrows the path forward toward approaches that are not dominated by a single axis of variation.
