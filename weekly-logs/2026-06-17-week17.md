# Overview

This week marked a shift from architecture experimentation toward grounding the model in the underlying physics of the printhead, picking up directly from last week's finding that a generic nozzle-axis CNN only partially respects the data's true spatial structure. Rather than jumping straight to the chip-level sequence model proposed last week, the focus turned to first understanding why individual chips might behave differently, so that any embedding or coupling structure added to the model reflects real physical causes rather than an arbitrary inductive bias.

A literature review identified six physical domains that could plausibly explain chip-to-chip variation: target electrical energy (captured by the dataset's voltage parameter), acoustic waveform shape (captured by flank ratio), and four currently unmeasured domains — manufacturing/geometric dimensions, thermal environment, spatial assembly alignment, and true electrical efficiency (electrical crosstalk/trace impedance). This grounding, drawn from piezo inkjet printhead literature, gives a physical basis for later deciding which traits an embedding layer should learn from rather than guessing at chip "strength" blindly.

This was then formalised into an explicit mathematical model: each nozzle's output is the sum of a per-nozzle baseline response `s(U_i)`, a spatial coupling term `ΣW_ij(θ)·s(U_j)` summed over neighbouring nozzles, and noise. The coupling weights `W_ij` are conditioned on the global settings `θ` (colour, coverage), capturing the idea that how strongly neighbouring nozzles interact may itself depend on process conditions.

Implementation proceeded in stages, with a new train/val/test split performed on unique (V, F_r, dt2) configurations to keep full operating-point groups together. First, the baseline term `s(U_i)` was implemented as a small MLP shared across nozzles, with hidden dimension swept across {4, 8, 16, 32, 64}; 8 hidden units gave the best test MSE (0.155), notably outperforming larger widths. Second, the coupling term `W_ij(θ)` was implemented as a hypernetwork: a small network maps `θ` to a local interaction kernel of radius K, with self-interaction explicitly masked out, applied via a per-sample grouped 1D convolution. Finally, two ways of combining these were built: a simpler version that projects global features into a spatial signal and refines it with a generic 1D CNN, and a more literal implementation of the formalism — a baseline net plus the coupling net, mixed through a learnable, exponentiated coupling-scale parameter to prevent the coupling term from dominating early in training.

## Reflection

Stepping back into the literature first made it clearer which physical mechanisms are plausible drivers of chip-to-chip variation, and which of those are even measurable with the current dataset.

Translating that into an explicit `s(U_i) + W_ij(θ)` formulation was also a useful forcing function: it made the assumptions of the model explicit and falsifiable, rather than leaving them implicit inside an architecture diagram. Building the baseline and coupling components separately, before combining them, made it possible to validate each piece  instead of debugging an end-to-end model. The unintuitive result that a smaller hidden dimension (8) outperformed larger ones is a useful signal that the baseline response itself may be simpler than expected, and that complexity is better spent on the coupling term than on the per-nozzle baseline.
