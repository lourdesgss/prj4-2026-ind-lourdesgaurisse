Following the failure of PCA as a dimensionality reduction strategy, this week stepped back from modelling and returned to the feature space itself to develop a clearer understanding of what the inputs actually represent and how they relate to one another. This analysis was carried out in [1.5-feature-analysis.ipynb](../evidence/notebooks/1.5-feature-analysis.pdf), with each feature examined individually and cross-tabulated against the others.

**`Color$`** is perfectly balanced across the dataset, with each of the four ink channels (C, M, Y, K) accounting for exactly 25% of rows. OHE encoding was confirmed as the appropriate choice since there is no ordinal relationship between colours.

**`V` and `F_r`** were found to be a composite feature rather than two independent inputs. Each voltage level has exactly 4–5 paired `F_r` values representing a resonance sweep, and `F_r = 1.02` acts as a shared baseline tested across all voltages. This has a direct implication for modelling: treating `V` and `F_r` as independent continuous inputs is misleading.

**`dt2`** has 5 discrete levels, perfectly balanced and fully independent of all other features. It can be safely treated as a standalone input without interaction terms.

**`Coverage#`** has 30 discrete levels and is also pre-set and independent, with consistent distribution across colour channels.

**`HeadIdx#`** — the print head chip index — produced the most structurally significant finding of the analysis. Most chips have 3,600 sample rows, but chips 5–12 are consistently missing waveform configurations corresponding to `V=22` and `V=24` operating points, and also have fewer `dt2` combinations tested. Chip 19 is missing one additional configuration. Critically, the missing configurations are not random: they are clustered in a contiguous block of chip indices, suggesting a physical explanation related to the spatial layout of chips on the printhead.

This observation reframed `HeadIdx#` from a simple categorical identifier to a spatially structured variable. Chips adjacent in index are likely adjacent physically, and the fact that a specific contiguous range shares identical data gaps points to chip-to-chip correlation that a standard independent-feature model will not capture. 

This prompted an investigation into **spatial autoregressive modelling** as a framework for encoding the relationship between neighbouring chips directly into the model. A summary of relevant approaches is documented in [spatial-autoregressive-models.md](spatial-autoregressive-models.md).

The summary of the feature space produced at the end of the notebook consolidates these findings into a set of concrete modelling recommendations: stratify the train/test split by `HeadIdx#`; treat `V + F_r` as a composite operating point; consider tree-based models or embedding layers for the discrete inputs; and account for the spatial structure of `HeadIdx#` in whichever model is ultimately selected.

### Reflection

Returning to analysis after a failed modelling attempt proved more productive than pressing forward with alternative reduction methods. The PCA result from Week 11 was a symptom of an insufficiently understood feature space, and addressing that directly — rather than working around it — led to findings that will have lasting consequences for the model design.

The most significant shift this week was recognising `HeadIdx#` not as a nuisance variable to be encoded away, but as the primary carrier of spatial structure in the dataset. The clustered pattern of missing waveform configurations in chips 5–12 would be easy to overlook in a purely statistical summary, but becomes interpretable once the physical layout of the printhead is considered. This kind of domain-informed reasoning is difficult to apply without first understanding what the data represents at the hardware level.
