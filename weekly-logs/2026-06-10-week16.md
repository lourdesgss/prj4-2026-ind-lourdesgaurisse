# Overview

Building on last week's shift from analytical exploration toward implementation, this week was spent building and training a hybrid MLP-CNN model. The architecture pairs an MLP branch, which captures the global process-condition dependencies (V, Fr, Coverage, Color, dt2), with a CNN residual branch that learns local corrections along the nozzle axis, on the assumption that neighbouring nozzles are physically coupled and not independent.

Two iterations of the model were built. The first used a single shallow convolutional residual block. The second extended this with multi-scale kernels (5, 11, and 21 nozzles) to capture fine, medium, and broader neighbourhood interactions, along with batch normalisation, minibatch training via DataLoader, and a learning-rate scheduler to stabilise training on Apple Silicon (MPS).

A more rigorous data split was also introduced. Rather than splitting rows randomly, the split was performed on unique (V, Fr) operating point combinations, with all rows belonging to the same configuration assigned to a single side. This produces a genuine group split that tests generalisation to unseen operating conditions, rather than interpolation within already-seen ones, which a row-level split would have allowed.

After 190 epochs, the hybrid model reached a test MAE of 0.0249 — an 82% reduction relative to the plain MLP baseline (0.139), though still short of the XGBoost + PCA baseline (0.018). Inspecting predicted vs. actual waveforms pointed to a more fundamental issue behind the modest CNN gains: the dataset isn't a complete, uniform 30-chip grid per condition (some conditions only have 22–30 chips), so convolving along the nozzle axis only partially respects the data's real spatial structure, while the chip axis was effectively being ignored entirely.

This reframes the next step. Rather than continuing to refine a nozzle-axis CNN, the plan going forward is to model the true hierarchy explicitly: each chip treated as a structured 1,120-dimensional unit, and the printhead as an ordered, sometimes incomplete, sequence of up to 30 chips. The next architecture will need a chip-level encoder followed by a sequence model over the chip axis, with masking for missing chips, so spatial relationships between adjacent chips are learned directly rather than assumed.

## Reflection

This week confirmed the spatial-field framing carried over from previous weeks, but in a more literal way: the nozzle-axis CNN did produce real but modest gains, and digging into why surfaced a structural problem with how the chip axis had been treated.

It also reframed how I'm thinking about "improvement." An 82% MAE reduction over the MLP baseline is meaningful, but it's clear that adding convolution along the wrong axis has a ceiling, and that ceiling is structural rather than something more training or tuning would fix. Moving toward a chip-sequence architecture feels like a more honest match to the actual shape of the problem, rather than a further refinement of an approach that was already misaligned with the data.
